bees

When we talk about bees most people think of a honey bee, but there are many different types of other bees which have never been domesticated. Some live in nests underground -- like the bumblebee -- while others make hives like the honey bee. Some look like flies, some look like wasps, and others look completely different.

queen

Each honey bee hive has only one queen bee. Workers select a normal worker brood to become a queen, and feed her royal jelly to make her different. She may live up to 3 years, depending on the species. Many beekeepers will locate and mark the queen with a colorful dot or a number on her back to easily identify her among her loyal subjects.

worker

It's likely that every bee you've ever seen was a worker, unless you have looked inside a hive. The workers are all female, and are responsible for hive maintenance, insulation, and construction, and foraging for nectar, pollen, and water.

drone

There are never very many drones in a hive. They don't do any work other than mating with the queen, and don't live very long. They are a bit bigger than the workers, but smaller than the queen.

comb

Worker bees create the honeycomb that is then used to store eggs, larvae, pollen, nectar, and honey. You can eat the honeycomb whole or get the honey out with a centrifuge or manual crushing. Beeswax is stuck to natural surfaces (like trees and culverts) with a resin-like substance produced by worker bees called propolis.

pollen

Worker bees collect pollen from plants while they are gathering nectar. Their legs act like little pollen baskets because the fine hairs catch and hold the pollen grains. The pollen forms small pellets in the comb and is used for allergy treatments and boosting immune functioning.

honey

Honey is a wonderful substance that is a natural preservative, antifungal, antibacterial, and a natural source of sugar. Bees make it so they have a source of food over the winter when there are no flowering plants. Bees in tropical climates make less honey and produce more young instead.

pollination

Many world-wide food crops depend on bees for pollination. When bees go out to forage for food, they only visit one type of plant per trip. As bees visit different plants to get nectar, they pick up and drop off pollen at each plant they visit, pollinating the plants. Almonds, apples, broccoli, cucumber, peach, sugar beet, and vanilla are just some of the crops that rely on bees for pollination.
